Reactive attenuators — a tutorial

From textbook L-pads to the limits of human hearing. The running example is a Fender Blues Jr IV driving a Jensen C12N through the Hewitt M2 — but the principles transfer to any amp / speaker / attenuator combination.

1 · The textbook attenuator

Pretend, for a moment, that the world is simple. An ideal amplifier — zero output impedance, infinite bandwidth — drives a resistive attenuator into a resistive load (an "ideal speaker" that's just a fixed resistor). The voltage at the load is a constant fraction of the amp's output, independent of frequency.

The math is one line: H = (Rshunt ∥ RL) / (Rseries + Rshunt ∥ RL). Pick the resistors for whatever attenuation you want and the frequency response is a flat horizontal line.

Insight: if frequency-independent components drive a frequency-independent load, attenuation is just a number. The rest of this tutorial is about why neither assumption holds in practice.

2 · Speakers aren't resistors

A guitar speaker is a mass-on-spring with a voice coil in a magnet gap. It is emphatically not a resistor — its impedance swings dramatically with frequency, and that swing is most of what gives a speaker its character.

Three features dominate. At DC the impedance is the voice coil's DC resistance Re ≈ 6 Ω. Around 100–150 Hz the cone's mechanical resonance Fs creates a peak that can reach 4–6× Re. Above ~2 kHz the voice coil's inductance Le drives the impedance up monotonically.

Now consider the simplest possible "attenuator": one resistor in series, no shunt. The voltage at the speaker is Vamp · Zspkr / (Rseries + Zspkr). Where Zspkr is high (resonance, HF rise), the series resistor drops less of the voltage and more gets through. Where Zspkr is low (the dip between resonance and the HF rise), the series resistor drops more. The "attenuation" bumps and dips with the impedance curve.

Insight: a single resistor is not a flat attenuator into a real speaker — it applies the speaker's impedance curve as an EQ. This is why crude "stick a resistor in line" attenuators get a reputation for sounding boomy and uneven.

3 · Tube amps aren't voltage sources

A power amp is not an ideal voltage source. The output stage has finite source impedance Ramp, and the ratio Rload / Ramp is called the damping factor — higher values mean more voltage-source-like behavior.

Solid-state amps usually run damping factors above 100. Tube amps with global negative feedback land between 5 and 15. Tube amps without feedback (Class-A designs, smaller combos like the Blues Jr) can be down at 1–3, where Ramp is comparable to the speaker's nominal impedance.

The amp's source impedance interacts with the speaker's impedance curve. Where Zspkr rises (resonance, HF), the speaker pulls less current; the higher Ramp, the more this lightened current draw shows up as a voltage bump at the speaker terminals. A "tight" damping factor flattens this; a "loose" one accentuates it.

Insight: what the player hears isn't just what comes out of the tubes — it's the tubes into that specific speaker, with the source-impedance dance baked in. The same amp into a flat dummy load gives a measurably different result.

4 · The reference

Combine sections 2 and 3. The voltage delivered to a real speaker by a real amp, no attenuator in the path:

Href(f) = Zspkr(f) / (Ramp + Zspkr(f))

This is not flat. It has the resonance peak, the HF rise, and the amp-speaker coupling all in there. Crucially: this is what the player and the band have been listening to all along. It's the sound.

A transparent attenuator should deliver K · Href(f) to the speaker — same shape, scaled down by a constant K. The amp should "sound the same, just quieter".

Insight: "flat attenuation response" is the wrong design goal. A genuinely flat-response attenuator would strip out the very character that makes the amp sound like the amp.

5 · Reactive matching — the M2

The Hewitt M2 (and most attenuators marketed as "reactive") pursue two goals at once:

  1. The transfer function HM2(f) into the speaker should track K · Href(f) — preserve the shape.
  2. The input impedance Zin,M2(f) the amp sees should track Zspkr(f) — so the amp "sees" the same load it would normally see.

Stage 1's L1 + R2B + R1 branch is the main mechanism. As frequency rises, L1's reactance increases, raising the shunt branch's impedance — mimicking how a real speaker's |Z| rises at HF. The series R2A and the cascaded L-pad stages set the overall attenuation level.

The M2's input impedance tracks the speaker's shape — same resonance peak, similar HF rise. Now compare the transfer function against a pure-resistive L-pad with the same DC attenuation:

Insight: the reactive components aren't there for marketing. They're load-aware compensation — correcting for the fact that the speaker presents a complex impedance. A purely resistive attenuator hides the speaker; a reactive one preserves it.

6 · Your ears

There's one final limit even a perfect attenuator can't fix: the listener.

Human hearing is not frequency-flat, and worse, its frequency response changes with sound pressure level. Lower-SPL listening makes the ear progressively less sensitive to the frequency extremes — especially bass.

The Fletcher-Munson curves (modernized as ISO 226) describe this. Each contour shows the actual SPL needed at each frequency to produce the same perceived loudness, in phons.

At 100 phon ("cranked"), the contour is nearly flat — bass, mids, and treble are heard roughly equally. At 40 phon ("quiet bedroom"), the ear is ~30 dB less sensitive at 50 Hz than at 1 kHz. So even with a perfectly transparent attenuator, the same audio at lower SPL sounds bass-and-treble light to the ear.

This is psychoacoustics, not the attenuator. Some attenuators add a mild low-shelf and/or treble boost ("compensation EQ") to push back against this perceptual rolloff at low SPLs. The M2 doesn't — it leaves that judgment to the player.

Insight: "same sound, just quieter" is perceptually impossible. Knowing why helps separate what an attenuator can fix (load matching, transfer function shape) from what it can't (your ears at low SPL).
