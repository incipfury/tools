Reactive attenuators — a tutorial

Two parts. Part One is the everyday amp-and-speaker system — the rig exactly as it ships from the store — and why it sounds the way it does. Part Two adds an attenuator and works out what it takes to turn that sound down without losing it. The running example is a Fender Blues Jr IV driving a Jensen C12N through the Hewitt M2, but the principles transfer to any amp / speaker / attenuator combination. No math required: every idea is backed by a plot you can read at a glance.

Start here · the system you already own

An electric guitar rig is two halves: an amplifier and a speaker; and that pairing has a voice that comes from the two of them pushing against each other.

Part One looks at that untouched system — how a speaker behaves as an electrical load, how an amplifier behaves driving one, and what the two produce together. Part Two then adds the attenuator. Once Part One has pinned down what the untouched system does, it's clear what an attenuator has to preserve — and why the obvious cheap approach falls short.

Insight: you can't judge what an attenuator should do until you know what the untouched rig already does. So we build the system first, then we touch it.

Reference · the cheat sheet

A handful of symbols and units recur throughout. You never have to calculate with any of these — they're just labels on the curves — but it helps to know what they mean when you spot them.

Units
Ω
Ohms — a measure of electrical resistance or impedance. A higher value means the component "pushes back" harder against current flow.
H, mH
Henries (and milli-Henries; 1 mH = 0.001 H) — the unit of inductance. In Part Two, the inductor L1 is the key component used to imitate the physical voice coil of a speaker.
Hz, kHz
Hertz — frequency, or pitch. Low Hz is bass; high kHz is treble. The plots sweep 20 Hz–20 kHz, roughly the span of human hearing.
dB
Decibels — a ratio scale for loudness or gain. "−12 dB" is a sizeable cut; "0 dB" means no change at all.
phon
A unit of perceived loudness — it shows up in Part Two, when we talk about your ears rather than the circuit.
Symbols on the plots
H( f )
The transfer function — a stage's "EQ fingerprint," explained in full in Section 3. Read it as: how some part of the signal chain reshapes the signal across frequency. Note this is a different "H" from Henries above; context always makes clear which is meant.
Z( f )
Impedance, shown as it changes with frequency. Zspkr is the speaker's impedance; Zin is what the amp "sees" looking into the attenuator.
Ramp
The amp's own internal source resistance — small, but for tube amps just big enough to matter.
K
The target attenuation amount — the single constant an ideal attenuator scales everything down by.
Node M
A specific labelled junction inside the M2 circuit (see Section 6) — the point in the signal path immediately after the primary inductor L1.
Speaker parameters (Thiele/Small)
fs
The frequency where the speaker cone naturally wants to vibrate the most — it causes a pronounced bass bump in the impedance curve.
Le
The voice coil's inductance — it makes impedance climb at high frequencies and shapes the speaker's treble roll-off.
Re
The voice coil's plain DC resistance — the "floor" of the impedance curve, what you'd read with a multimeter across the speaker terminals.
RES
The impedance at resonance — essentially how tall the fs bass bump rises.
Qms
The mechanical "quality factor" — whether that resonance peak is sharp and narrow or broad and rounded.
Part One
The amp-and-speaker system

The rig exactly as you'd buy it — no attenuator yet. What a speaker is, electrically; what an amp is, as a source; and what the two of them produce together.

1 · Speakers aren't resistors

A guitar speaker is a mass-on-spring with a voice coil in a magnet gap. It is emphatically not a resistor — its impedance swings dramatically with frequency, and that swing is most of what gives a speaker its character.

The plot above sweeps five real drivers: two 8 Ω speakers (a Jensen C12N and a Celestion Greenback), two 16 Ω speakers (a Celestion G10N-40 and a 16 Ω Vintage 30), and, dashed, the 4 Ω curve from two C12Ns wired in parallel. Every one of them has the same three-part anatomy.

At the low end the impedance settles toward the voice coil's plain DC resistance Re — roughly 6 Ω for a typical 8 Ω driver, roughly double that for a 16 Ω one. That's the number you'd read with a multimeter across the terminals. Around 75–150 Hz the cone's mechanical resonance Fs throws up a tall peak, often 4–6× Re. And above ~2 kHz the voice coil's inductance Le drags the impedance steadily upward.

So "nominal impedance" is loose: the "8 Ω" and "16 Ω" labels are closer to an average than a measurement — none of these curves sits flat at its rated value anywhere across the band. And the curves are stackable: two 8 Ω drivers in parallel give the 4 Ω trace, the identical shape at half the impedance.

Insight: every speaker presents a fingerprint-shaped impedance curve, and the rated impedance is just a label slapped on something that's moving the whole time. This wandering curve is the load that everything downstream — amp, and later attenuator — has to cope with.

2 · The amp is not a voltage source

A power amp is not an ideal voltage source. The output stage has finite source impedance Ramp, and the ratio Rload / Ramp is called the damping factor — higher values mean more voltage-source-like behavior.

Solid-state amps usually run damping factors above 100. Tube amps with global negative feedback land between 5 and 15. Tube amps without feedback — Class-A designs, small combos — can be down at 1–3, where Ramp is comparable to the speaker's nominal impedance.

The amp's source impedance interacts with the speaker's impedance curve. Where Zspkr rises (resonance, HF), the speaker pulls less current; the higher Ramp, the more that lightened current draw shows up as a voltage bump at the speaker terminals. A "tight" damping factor flattens this; a "loose" one accentuates it. The plot uses one speaker, but the same dance plays out with every driver from Section 1.

Insight: what the player hears isn't just what comes out of the tubes — it's the tubes into that specific speaker, with the source-impedance dance baked in. The same amp into a flat dummy load gives a measurably different result.

3 · The reference — what you've been hearing

Combine the last two sections. The voltage delivered to a real speaker by a real amp, with nothing else in the path:

Href(f) = Zspkr(f) / (Ramp + Zspkr(f))

Meet H( ) — the "EQ fingerprint"

You'll see the letter H throughout this tutorial — Href here, HM2 later, plain H on the analyzer plots. Formally it's a "transfer function"; think of it as an EQ fingerprint — a description of how some piece of the signal chain reshapes the signal across frequency, which frequencies pass through strong and which get dampened.

No math needed: whenever H( ) comes up, just read the plotted curve. H( ) is the engine that calculates that curve's shape.

Every H( ) runs between an input and an output. For Href here, the input is the raw power off the amplifier's output transformer and the output is the voltage reaching the speaker terminals. In Part Two we'll talk about the attenuator's own H( ) the same way — what happens to the tone between its input and output jacks.

Href(f) is not flat. It carries the resonance peak, the HF rise, and the amp-speaker coupling all at once — and this is what the player and the band have been hearing all along. It's the sound.

Here Href is plotted against K · Href(f) — the same curve scaled straight down by a constant K. That scaled-down version is the goal for Part Two: an attenuator delivering K·Href would "sound the same, just quieter." Keep this curve's shape in mind — every plot in Part Two is really just asking whether the attenuated signal still looks like it.

Insight: the amp-and-speaker system you own already has a strongly shaped response. That shape is the tone — which means a "flat" attenuator would be the wrong tool. Anything we put in the path has to preserve this curve, not erase it.
Part Two
Putting an attenuator in the path

An attenuator burns off most of the amp's power as heat so you can run the rig quietly. The whole question is how it burns that power off — and whether the sound from Part One survives the trip.

4 · The textbook attenuator

Pretend, for a moment, that the world is simple. An ideal amplifier — zero output impedance, infinite bandwidth — drives a resistive attenuator into a resistive load (an "ideal speaker" that's just a fixed resistor). The voltage at the load is a constant fraction of the amp's output, independent of frequency.

The math is one line — the attenuator's own transfer function H( ), the EQ fingerprint from Section 3 applied to this little network: H = (Rshunt ∥ RL) / (Rseries + Rshunt ∥ RL). Pick the resistors for whatever attenuation you want and the response is a flat horizontal line.

Insight: if frequency-independent components drive a frequency-independent load, attenuation is just a number — the flat line on the plot above. But Part One already demolished both assumptions: the speaker is not a resistor, and the amp is not ideal. So the real question is what this tidy little divider does once it's dropped into an actual rig.

5 · Why a plain resistor isn't enough

The textbook attenuator assumed a resistive load. Drop that assumption — swap in the real speaker curves from Section 1 — and even the simplest attenuator stops behaving.

Consider the crudest possible "attenuator": one resistor in series, no shunt. The voltage at the speaker is Vamp · Zspkr / (Rseries + Zspkr). Where Zspkr is high (resonance, HF rise), the series resistor drops less of the voltage and more gets through. Where Zspkr is low (the dip between resonance and the HF rise), it drops more. The "attenuation" bumps and dips along with the impedance curve.

And here's the key turn: you can't fix this by adding more resistors. A resistor network, however cleverly arranged, is still frequency-blind — it cannot help but stamp the speaker's impedance curve onto the signal. To track a moving target — the speaker's wandering impedance — the attenuator needs components that move too. That is the case for going reactive.

Insight: a single resistor is not a flat attenuator into a real speaker — it applies the speaker's impedance curve as an EQ. This is why crude "stick a resistor in line" attenuators get a reputation for sounding boomy and uneven, and why a serious attenuator has to be more than resistors.

6 · Reactive matching — the M2

A reactive attenuator answers Section 5 with inductors (and sometimes capacitors) — components whose effective "resistance" deliberately changes with frequency. The Hewitt M2, like most attenuators marketed as "reactive," uses them to pursue two goals at once:

  1. The transfer function HM2(f) into the speaker should track K · Href(f) — preserve the shape from Section 3.
  2. The input impedance Zin,M2(f) the amp sees should track Zspkr(f) — so the amp "sees" the same kind of load it would normally see.

Before the specific resistor values, here's the roadmap. The M2 is a four-stage L-pad ladder — four cascading steps, each one padding the signal down a little further:

Stage 1≈ −7 dB in · 0 dB bypassed
The reactive, compensated stage — the one with the inductor L1. It has no individual switch among SW1–3, so it's in circuit whenever the attenuator is engaged, giving the amp's output transformer a safe, speaker-like reactive load. The True Bypass 4PDT switch is what removes it, lifting the whole attenuator out at once (Section 8) — so Stage 1 is either ≈−7 dB (engaged) or 0 dB (bypassed), never independently switched. It's both the safety floor and the tone-shaping heart of the unit.
Stage 2switchable · ≈ −14 dB
The first switchable step, and the biggest single cut — a series-plus-shunt L-pad. On its own it takes you most of the way from gig volume down toward conversation volume.
Stage 3switchable · ≈ −7 dB
A second switchable resistive step — a smaller L-pad cut. Flip it in to pad down further; flip it out and the signal passes straight through.
Stage 4switchable · ≈ −3.5 dB
The fine-trim step — a small final cut for dialing in the exact bedroom level. All four stages stacked reach roughly −31.5 dB.

So Stage 1 carries the tone and protects the amp; Stages 2–4 are the cascading volume steps you switch to reach bedroom levels. The True Bypass switch sits above all of them — engage it and every stage, Stage 1 included, drops out at once.

Inside Stage 1, the L1 + R2B + R1 branch is the main mechanism. The schematic labels its internal junction Node M — the point just after L1, where R2B and the shunt resistor R1 meet. As frequency rises, L1's reactance increases, raising that branch's impedance — mimicking how a real speaker's |Z| rises at HF. R2A and the cascaded L-pad stages set the overall attenuation level.

Why the circuit feels "alive"

A volume pedal or a cheap resistive attenuator throws a static blanket over your tone — the same dampening at every frequency. The transfer function here is dynamic instead.

The reactive components effectively "listen" to the amp: when it pushes a low-end thump or a high-end squeal, L1 and its branch shift their effective resistance on the fly, matching what a real voice coil would have done. That's what keeps the amp feeling alive under your fingers instead of choked.

The M2's input impedance tracks the speaker's shape — same resonance peak, similar HF rise. Now compare the transfer function against a pure-resistive L-pad with the same DC attenuation:

The M2 closely follows the K·Href target. The pure-resistive L-pad deviates — it's flatter in absolute response, which is exactly the wrong thing if the goal is preserving the speaker's character.

One practical wrinkle: the M2 is built around an 8 Ω speaker, but a 16 Ω cabinet can be run with a small modification — a 68 Ω shunt resistor (here R10, from Node M to ground, in parallel with R1). It pulls the overall impedance the amp sees back into the expected range, keeping the amp safely matched to the higher-impedance load.

Insight: the reactive components aren't there for marketing. They're load-aware compensation — correcting for the fact that the speaker presents a complex impedance. A purely resistive attenuator hides the speaker; a reactive one preserves it.

7 · Your ears

There's one final limit even a perfect attenuator can't fix: the listener.

Human hearing is not frequency-flat, and worse, its frequency response changes with sound pressure level. Lower-SPL listening makes the ear progressively less sensitive to the frequency extremes — especially bass.

The Fletcher-Munson curves (modernized as ISO 226) describe this. Each contour shows the actual SPL needed at each frequency to produce the same perceived loudness, in phons.

At 100 phon ("cranked"), the contour is nearly flat — bass, mids, and treble are heard roughly equally. At 40 phon ("quiet bedroom"), the ear is ~30 dB less sensitive at 50 Hz than at 1 kHz. So even with a perfectly transparent attenuator, the same audio at lower SPL sounds bass-and-treble light to the ear.

This is psychoacoustics, not the attenuator. Some attenuators add a mild low-shelf and/or treble boost ("compensation EQ") to push back against this perceptual rolloff at low SPLs; others leave that judgment to the player. It's a design choice, separate from the load-matching job the rest of this tutorial is about.

Insight: "same sound, just quieter" is perceptually impossible. Knowing why helps separate what an attenuator can fix (load matching, transfer function shape) from what it can't (your ears at low SPL).

8 · Bypass — the attenuator removed

It's worth being clear about one limiting case: the signal path with the attenuator taken out entirely. Many attenuators — the M2 among them — include a bypass switch that does exactly this, routing the amp straight to the speaker as if the box weren't there; some DIY builds skip it. Either way, "bypassed" is a useful reference state to understand.

Bypassed, the signal chain is just amp-into-speaker again — so the transfer function is Href(f), the bare reference curve from Section 3. That's what makes it the natural baseline: every attenuated curve in Part Two is really being measured against this one. The analyzer's True Bypass control does the same thing — flip it and the plotted response collapses back to Href.

Insight: bypass isn't a setting so much as a frame of reference — the "0 dB, attenuator absent" case that every other curve in this tutorial is compared to.
