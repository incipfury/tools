Photonic Assisted Radio-Frequency Interference Mitigation

Vincent J. Urick, Modesto E. Godinez, and Dennis C. Mikeska
Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), Arlington, VA · ECS Federal · 2 Circle Inc.
Citation V. J. Urick, M. E. Godinez, and D. C. Mikeska, “Photonic Assisted Radio-Frequency Interference Mitigation,” Journal of Lightwave Technology, vol. 38, no. 6, pp. 1268–1274, Mar. 2020. doi: 10.1109/JLT.2019.2955921

Rapidly evolving technologies for photonic interference mitigation are surveyed and organized into three architectural categories: photonic RF filters, photonic RF cancellers, and photonic RF front ends. Analysis is presented on a layered approach that combines linear and nonlinear photonic RF front ends within a single Mach–Zehnder modulator (MZM) architecture, and an argument is made for cooperative photonic, analog-electronic, and digital-electronic interference mitigation strategies.

Full-duplex; interference cancellation, mitigation, and suppression; microwave photonics; radio-frequency photonics; simultaneous transmit and receive (STAR).

I. Background and Motivation

Modern RF systems face increasingly demanding interference environments arising from electronic protection, full-duplex radio, and other simultaneous transmit/receive (STAR) requirements. The most challenging applications require self-interference suppression well in excess of 100 dB at operational frequencies extending into the millimeter wave with instantaneous bandwidths exceeding 1 GHz. No singular technique meets these requirements, motivating layered solutions that combine analog electronic, digital electronic, and—as argued in this work—photonic processing layers.

Photonics offers complementary capability that conventional electronics cannot match in the same form factor: very low fiber propagation loss (0.2 dB/km), instantaneous bandwidths beyond 10 GHz, and analog signal processing that introduces low latency and operates without prior knowledge of interferer waveforms. Conventional electronic cancellers, by contrast, typically provide less than 30 dB of suppression near 1 GHz of instantaneous bandwidth.

II. State of the Art

The paper organizes the published literature into three architectural categories defined by their insertion point in the RF chain (Fig. 2 of the paper):

A.  Photonic RF Filters

Inserted between the receive and transmit paths, these implement filtering directly in the optical domain. Subcategories include fiber-based finite- and infinite-impulse-response notch filters, filters cooperating with digital signal processing, and filters based on stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS). The principal limitation is a noise figure of 20–40 dB with commercially available technology, which often outweighs the bandwidth advantage versus electronic filters.

B.  Photonic RF Cancellers

Photonic cancellers sample the transmit waveform, route it through a bank of fiber-optic delay lines configured as an FIR filter, and recombine it with the receive path to subtract coherent self-interference. They inherit the low loss and wide bandwidth of fiber but face two principal challenges: tap scalability (each tap is hardware, not memory) and RF-domain phase control given the ~2×104 ratio between optical and microwave frequencies. Photonic integrated circuits and photonic-lantern fiber combiners are emerging to address both.

C.  Photonic RF Front Ends

These leverage the electro-optic modulator—typically a LiNbO3 MZM—directly as the mitigation element. Two regimes are distinguished:

  • Linear regime (e.g., the TIPRx counter-propagating architecture[37]): exploits signal-path symmetry through the MZM electrodes to achieve high transmit-to-receive isolation.
  • Nonlinear regime (Bessel-function nulling[45]): drives the MZM into compression so that high-power interference falls on a periodic zero of the modulator response, while lower-power signals of interest remain in the linear region.

Front-end techniques produced the highest reported suppression and the widest instantaneous bandwidth among all surveyed photonic methods.

III. Layered Optical Approach

Section III of the paper presents a single-aperture architecture (Fig. 6) that combines the linear and nonlinear front-end techniques in a single MZM. The receive path traverses the modulator biased for the nonlinear regime, nulling high-power self-interference; the counter-propagating transmit path is isolated from the receive path via the linear technique and is unaffected by the nonlinear suppression because of its differing phase relationship.

Combining Eqs. (2)–(3) of [45] with Eq. (12) of [37], the paper derives a closed-form expression for the combined isolation:

ISO  =  c6 Vπ2 sin2(2πfnL/c) J12(πVint/Vπ) 16π8 Vint2 f6 n6 L6 J02(πVint/Vπ) (1)

Equation (1) holds under five assumptions: (i) the MZM has a tapered electrode-to-waveguide interaction optimized for the linear technique; (ii) the MZM electrodes are lossless; (iii) Vπ is the same for the signal and interference; (iv) impedances are well matched throughout; and (v) the signal voltage is much less than Vπ/π. For a fixed interference voltage, Vπ dictates both the magnitude and the frequency response of the isolation, so effective tuning of Vπ controls the suppression profile.

Plotting Eq. (1) with parameters drawn from [37] and [45] (n = 2.2, L = 7 cm, Vπ = 4.0 V at 6 GHz rising linearly to 4.6 V at 18 GHz, Vint = 24.5 dBm) yields a maximum theoretical suppression near 13 GHz exceeding 100 dB. The measured combination of the two techniques (Fig. 8 of the paper) reaches 80–90 dB across 6–18 GHz, the difference reflecting that neither technique reached its theoretical minimum in laboratory practice.

V. Summary

Photonic mitigation techniques achieve their advantage in operational frequency range and instantaneous bandwidth rather than peak suppression at a single frequency—layered electronic techniques can achieve higher rejection over much narrower bands. The most promising published results combine linear and nonlinear MZM-based front-end techniques, yielding suppression in excess of 80 dB across multi-GHz bandwidths. The paper’s central recommendation is that successful interference mitigation systems treat photonics as part of a layered solution—working cooperatively with analog and digital electronics—rather than as a replacement for electronic hardware.
